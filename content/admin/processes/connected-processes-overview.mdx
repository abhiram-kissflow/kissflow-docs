# Connected processes overview

<PlanBadge plans={["enterprise"]} />

In Kissflow, you can connect multiple processes so that actions in one process automatically initiate related items in another. When a work item in a source process reaches a step you've configured, a dependent item is created in the connected (destination) process — carrying over the field values you've mapped between them. This lets team managers and IT admins orchestrate and keep related workflows across departments in sync, without manual handoffs.

## Why connect processes

- **Automate item creation** — when a source item reaches a configured step, a dependent item is created in the connected process automatically, cutting out manual handoffs between teams.
- **Coordinate across teams** — connected processes give everyone a shared, holistic view of how work moves between departments.
- **Reduce errors** — automating the handoff removes the chance of a step being missed or details being miscommunicated when work passes from one team to another.

<Callout type="info">
  What happens to the source item once its dependent item is completed — whether it's automatically unblocked and resumed, or handled another way — depends on how the connected step is configured. The example below describes the common setup, where the source item pauses at the connected step until its dependent item completes.
</Callout>

## Example: connecting Purchase Request and Purchase Order

Say your organization wants purchase orders to be created automatically once a purchase request is approved.

1. An employee submits a request in the **Purchase Request** process, filling in details like the software needed, quantity, and justification for the purchase.
2. The request moves through its approval stages. Once approved, it reaches a step named **Ready for order**.
3. Because **Ready for order** is configured as a connected step, a dependent item is automatically created in the **Purchase Order** process as soon as the source item reaches it. Field values you've mapped between the two processes — such as software name, quantity, and cost — carry over to the new item.
4. The procurement team picks up the new item in **Purchase Order**, reviews and finalizes the details, and sends it to the supplier.
5. Once the dependent item in **Purchase Order** is completed, the source item in **Purchase Request** resumes and moves on to its next step.
